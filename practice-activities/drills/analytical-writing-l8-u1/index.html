Your Targeted Drill System

Eleven weeks of deliberate writing practice, one week for each lesson of the unit. Every week sets a small loop of drills that sharpen exactly what that lesson taught, feeding the one essay you plan, draft, and redraft for the end-of-unit assessment. This page explains how it all works.

01

What this instrument is

Your course gives you three surfaces. The workbook is where each skill is taught and practiced, your primary practice surface. The supplements, the interactive tools and reference pages your teacher points to, are the second. This drill system is the third: the closing consolidation segment of each lesson, run with your teacher, and yours to continue at home between lessons.

One artifact carries the unit, the analytical essay you write on the AI prompt. The drills exist to feed it. Weeks 1 to 6 build the skills one by one, and from Week 7 every drill points at the live essay itself.

03

The weekly loop

Each week prescribes a small set of tasks, usually two or three drills with one review from an earlier week. The tasks follow the unit spine: the claim in Week 1, the warrant in Week 3, the full paragraph in Week 4, the essay from Week 7, your draft in Week 9, and a light warm-up only on assessment day.

The drills scale. Writing targets grow from single sentences in Week 1 to timed paragraphs by Week 5 and full-draft rounds by Week 10, the same way your essay grows. Earlier drills stay open below the week card, run any of them again whenever a part feels soft.

One thing is deliberate: the drill topics are never the topics your lesson just used. You drill the move on fresh ground, so the skill transfers instead of the example repeating. And each time you return, every drill opens on a new prompt.

04

The redraft loop

From Week 9 your full draft exists, and the redraft loop at the foot of the drill library opens. Its six questions are the six criteria of your assessment rubric, asked as working questions. Work the questions first, do not start rewriting until you have answered them. Run as many rounds as you choose, each round earns XP, and log every round on the session record.

05

XP, saving, and the scorecard

Every minute you invest earns one XP. Timers run per drill and per redraft round, and your session total shows in the top right.

This tool saves your progress in this browser: your name, your week, your ticks, your all-time XP, your drill attempts and their histories, your kept lines, and your redraft draft are all here when you come back on the same device. Two honest limits. The save lives in this browser on this device only, and clearing the browser's data clears it. So the printed session record remains the record that counts: print it and log your XP on your scorecard every session, and the save becomes a convenience, never a single point of failure.

06

A few practical notes

The Read-Back is the one drill you do aloud. Read your writing at speaking pace, then tick against what you actually heard, not what you meant to write. A sentence you stumble on twice is a sentence to rewrite.

The session record at the foot of the page has a reset control if this device is shared or a new student takes over. Press the question mark in the header at any time to return to this page.

The drill library

How the drills work

Drills 1 to 7 are write-and-check. Each isolates one part of the argument, and every attempt feeds the essay you are writing.

  • Pick a drill, start its timer, write your attempt, then check it against the standard under the drill name.
  • Press New prompt to draw a fresh version. Prompts marked Essay drill point at your live essay.
  • Drill 8, The Read-Back, is the one you do aloud, rehearse-and-check, ticked against what you heard.
  • Every minute earns one XP. Your progress saves in this browser, and the printed record is still the ledger.
Drill 1

The Arguable Claim

What good looks like: A position with a verb a reasonable person could oppose, not a fact you could check or a taste you could shrug at.
Turn this topic into an arguable claim, then write the fact sentence and the taste sentence you are refusing to write: school recycling programs.
A strong attempt
The school should replace its annual sports day with a term-long inter-house league, because a single day rewards the already athletic while a league builds habits.
A weak attempt
I think sports day is maybe not the best use of a school day.
The weak version hedges twice and names no position a person could oppose. The strong version stakes a claim, names its ground, and invites disagreement.

Drill 2

Evidence, Chosen and Interpreted

What good looks like: Two pieces relevant to the exact claim, each followed by an interpretation sentence that says what it shows.
For the claim 'schools should remove junk food from vending machines', write two strong pieces of evidence and one weak piece you would cut, with a one-line reason for the cut.
A strong attempt
In the two years after our library added a quiet-hours policy, average homework completion rose from 71 to 84 percent. This shows the barrier was noise, not effort, which supports the claim that study conditions, not motivation, drive completion.
A weak attempt
Lots of students say the library is better now.
The weak version is vague hearsay with no interpretation. The strong version is specific, and the second sentence does the analytical work of saying what the evidence shows.

Drill 3

The Warrant

What good looks like: The bridge stated in one sentence, why this evidence supports this claim, and it survives one honest objection.
Claim: public transit should be free for students. Evidence: in cities that dropped student fares, more teenagers traveled to libraries and museums. Write the warrant, then write one objection to it and answer in one line.
A strong attempt
Free student fares increased museum visits, therefore transit cost was blocking access, because when a barrier falls and behavior changes, the barrier was doing the blocking.
A weak attempt
The evidence obviously proves the claim.
The weak version asserts a connection instead of stating one. The strong version names the bridge in a form an opponent can inspect and push against.

Drill 4

The Analytical Paragraph

What good looks like: Claim, evidence, interpretation, warrant, link. Five moves, each doing its one job, with the connectors in place.
Build a full analytical paragraph from this pair. Claim: the city should build more small parks. Evidence: neighborhoods with parks nearby see more daily exercise and more neighbors who know each other. Write the other three moves yourself.
A strong attempt
Small parks change how a neighborhood lives. [claim] Streets within a short walk of a park record more daily exercise and more neighbors who know each other by name. [evidence] That pattern shows parks working as meeting grounds, not just green space. [interpretation] When a shared place lowers the effort of meeting, people meet, so the benefit follows the park. [warrant] A city that wants healthier, better-connected streets should build the places that make both easy. [link]
A weak attempt
Parks are good. People like them. They have grass and benches. The city should build more because parks are nice for everyone.
The weak version repeats the claim in different clothes. The strong version gives each sentence one job and the jobs add up to an argument.

Drill 5

Analytical Reading

What good looks like: The claim, evidence, and warrant of the passage named correctly, with a verdict on whether the reasoning holds.
Map this argument, then give your verdict: Middle schools should keep a daily recess. Students who move during the day focus better in class. Better focus means better learning, so cutting recess to add lessons costs more than it gains.
A strong attempt
Claim: keep daily recess. Evidence: movement improves classroom focus. Warrant: better focus means better learning, so the trade of recess for lessons loses more than it gains. Verdict: the reasoning holds, though the evidence would be stronger with a source.
A weak attempt
The passage says recess is good and I agree with it.
The weak version reacts instead of mapping. The strong version names all three moves and then, separately, judges whether they hold.

Drill 6

Precision

What good looks like: Hedges cut, verbs sharpened, one action per sentence, and the claim keeps its burden.
Cut the hedges and sharpen the verb, keeping the burden: I think schools should maybe consider making PE count a bit more toward grades.
A strong attempt
PE should count toward grades on the same terms as any other subject.
A weak attempt
I think schools should maybe consider making PE count a bit more toward grades.
Four hedges deleted and the verb sharpened, yet the burden of the claim is unchanged. Precision is cutting cover, not adding volume.

Drill 7

Transitions and Cohesion

What good looks like: Moves the reader between paragraphs without a bump, naming the relationship between the two ideas.
Write a one-line transition from a paragraph about evidence quality into a paragraph about the opposing view.
A strong attempt
The evidence, then, is strong, but strength is not the same as being unanswerable, and the other side has one point that deserves a fair hearing.
A weak attempt
Also, some people disagree.
The weak version changes topic without naming the relationship. The strong version tells the reader exactly how the next paragraph relates to the last one.

Drill 8

The Read-Back

What good sounds like: Read aloud, it sounds like a person arguing. Every sentence speakable in one breath, nothing you stumble on twice.
Read your newest paragraph aloud once, at speaking pace, and mark every place you stumble.
Read your writing aloud at speaking pace, then tick against what you actually heard.
  • Every sentence could be said in one breath
  • No sentence made you stumble twice
  • The claim sounded like a position, not an apology
A strong attempt
Read aloud, every sentence fits in one breath, the thesis sounds like a position, and nothing snags twice.
A weak attempt
Read silently and skimmed, so the ear never caught the sentence that runs four lines without a landing place.
The read-back only works out loud at speaking pace. The ear catches what the eye forgives.

The Redraft Loop · your full essay

Redraft your full essay

Opens in Week 9, once your full draft exists. The essay is the artifact this unit anchors on. Sharpen it in rounds, not in one pass.
Before you redraft, work through these. They are your assessment rubric's six criteria, asked as questions. Do not start rewriting until you have answered them.
  1. Claim and Thesis. Is your thesis a position a reasonable person could oppose, and does it sit where a reader expects it, at the end of your introduction?
  2. Evidence and Interpretation. Find any evidence standing alone. Every quote or fact needs its interpretation sentence, what does this show?
  3. Reasoning and Warrant. Take your weakest paragraph and state its warrant in one sentence. If you cannot, the paragraph does not argue yet, write the warrant in.
  4. Structure and Cohesion. Read only your topic sentences, top to bottom. Do they tell the argument on their own?
  5. Counterargument Awareness. Where would a thoughtful reader push back hardest, and does the essay acknowledge that push anywhere?
  6. Language and Conventions. Hunt the hedges. Cut I think, maybe, and sort of, then read each sentence again and see what it now claims.
